# Record Clearing — Texas

Two completely different remedies. Users (and many websites) conflate them. Get this distinction right first.

## Expunction vs. non-disclosure — the structural difference

| Remedy | Statutory basis | What it does | Who's eligible |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Expunction** | TX CCP Ch. 55 | Destroys arrest records. Agencies must remove references. The user can lawfully deny the arrest happened. | Narrow — typically applies only to arrests that did *not* lead to a conviction (acquittal, dismissal, no charges filed, certain pardon situations). |
| **Order of non-disclosure** | TX Gov't Code Ch. 411 | Seals the record from public view but keeps it visible to law enforcement and some agencies. The user can lawfully deny in *most* employment contexts. | Broader — typically applies to certain misdemeanors after a waiting period. Some categories now qualify for automatic non-disclosure (§ 411.072). |

If the user says "expunge" but is describing a conviction they served time for, they probably mean non-disclosure. Don't correct them harshly; just frame the answer in terms of what's actually available.

## The eligibility worksheet

Run through these questions with the user in order. Each one narrows the path.

### Step 1 — was there a conviction?

> "First thing — were you actually convicted, or did the case end some other way (dismissed, deferred adjudication, found not guilty, no charges filed)?"

- **Convicted (jury or plea)** → expunction is generally not available; check non-disclosure below.
- **Deferred adjudication that you completed** → not a conviction for most purposes; check non-disclosure under § 411.072 or § 411.0735.
- **Dismissed, acquitted, no-billed, or no charges filed** → expunction under CCP Ch. 55 may be available; route to legal aid for petition help.

### Step 2 — what was the offense category?

> "Was it a felony or a misdemeanor? And do you remember the level (state jail felony, third degree, Class A/B misdemeanor)?"

Disqualifying categories for non-disclosure under § 411.074(b) include:
- Offenses requiring sex offender registration
- Family violence offenses
- Aggravated kidnapping
- Murder, capital murder
- Trafficking, injury to a child/elderly/disabled person
- Stalking, certain violations of protective orders

If the offense is in this list → non-disclosure is not available. Pivot to talking about employer-disclosure strategy and resources for navigating with a record visible.

### Step 3 — what was the disposition?

- **Deferred adjudication, successfully completed** → § 411.072 (automatic non-disclosure for many first-time misdemeanors) or § 411.0735 (petition-based for certain offenses) may apply.
- **Probation/community supervision, completed** → narrower set of misdemeanors eligible for petition.
- **Imprisonment, completed** → very narrow eligibility; mostly not available.

### Step 4 — has the waiting period elapsed?

The waiting periods vary by offense and disposition. Common patterns:
- Many misdemeanors under § 411.0725: 2 years after community supervision ends.
- Some misdemeanors under § 411.072 (automatic): no waiting period for certain first-time offenses other than family violence and a few others.
- Felony deferred adjudication eligible under § 411.0725: 5 years.

You generally cannot determine this from a phone conversation alone — court records are needed.

### Step 5 — any disqualifying intervening conduct?

Most non-disclosure paths require that the user has not been convicted of *any* offense (other than minor traffic) during the relevant period. Family violence convictions disqualify regardless of timing.

## The output of the worksheet

After running the four steps, give the user one of three outcomes:

### Outcome A — likely eligible

> "Based on what you told me, it sounds like [expunction / non-disclosure under § 411.0735] might apply to your situation. I'd want a lawyer to confirm because the eligibility rules have exceptions I can't check from here. Two good options:
> - [Texas RioGrande Legal Aid](https://www.trla.org) — free civil legal aid, 1-888-988-9996
> - [Lone Star Legal Aid](https://www.lonestarlegal.org) — for the Houston / Gulf Coast area, 1-800-733-8394
>
> They can pull your court records and tell you for sure."

### Outcome B — likely not eligible under current law

> "Based on what you told me, [expunction / non-disclosure] doesn't look like a fit because [specific reason from the worksheet]. That doesn't mean nothing can be done — a pardon is a different process, and some employers may take a 'letter of explanation' even when the record is visible. Would it help if I pointed you to fair-chance employers and the Federal Bonding Program?"

Route to `employment-pathway`.

### Outcome C — can't tell from here

> "Honestly, I can't tell from this conversation alone — the eligibility rules turn on details from your court records that I don't have. The right next step is a free legal aid consult: [Texas RioGrande Legal Aid] for most of Texas, or [Lone Star Legal Aid] for the Houston area. They'll pull your records and tell you for sure."

This is the most common outcome. Don't pretend otherwise.

## What you absolutely do not do

- **Do not promise an outcome.** "You can get this expunged" is not your call. Even attorneys hedge here. "It sounds like you may be eligible" + "a lawyer will need to confirm" is the framing.
- **Do not estimate cost or timeline.** Filing fees, attorney fees, and court timelines vary by county. Wrong numbers do real damage.
- **Do not coach the user on what to say in a petition.** That's legal advice and it's not your role.
- **Do not collapse the difference between expunction and non-disclosure.** They are different remedies with different effects on background checks.
